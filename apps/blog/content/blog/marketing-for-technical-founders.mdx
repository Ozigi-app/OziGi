> **TL;DR:** Technical founders cannot run standard marketing, because the audience they are trying to reach blocks ads at high rates, distrusts polished messaging, and prefers to evaluate things for itself. The playbook that works is the opposite of a campaign: be useful in public, teach instead of pitch, reach the right people directly, and keep one honest voice across your content and your outreach. The common mistake is hiring a generic marketer or buying ads aimed at people who installed an ad blocker years ago. This guide lays out what to do instead, and the lean weekly rhythm to run it.

Most technical founders can build almost anything and freeze the moment it is time to market it. That is not a character flaw. It is a mismatch between the standard marketing playbook and the audience you are actually trying to reach.

The usual advice, run ads, write hype, hire a marketer, optimize a funnel, was built for selling to general consumers and business buyers. It works poorly on developers and technical teams, who behave differently at every step. Here is a playbook built for the audience you actually have.

## Why Does Normal Marketing Fail on Technical Audiences?

Normal marketing fails on technical audiences because they actively filter it out and distrust what gets through. Ads do not reach them, and hype repels them. The two main tools of conventional marketing are the two things this audience rejects most.

Start with ads. Among software developers and IT professionals, ad blocker usage runs around 50 to 60%, far above the general population, according to [analysis of ad blocker impact on analytics](https://www.kissmetrics.io/blog/ad-blocker-analytics-impact). Traffic from developer communities like Hacker News and Reddit is the most likely of all to be blocked. So a large share of your budget aimed at developers reaches an ad blocker, not a developer, and your analytics will not even show you the gap.

Then there is the trust problem. Technical buyers evaluate tools by reading the docs, trying the free tier, and asking peers, not by believing a tagline. Polished, hype-heavy messaging reads to them as a warning sign, not a selling point. With 180 million developers now on GitHub, per [GitHub's 2025 Octoverse report](https://github.blog/news-insights/octoverse/octoverse-a-new-developer-joins-github-every-second-as-ai-leads-typescript-to-1/), the audience is enormous, but it is an audience that markets to itself and treats your claims as something to verify.

## What Actually Works for Technical Founders?

What works is being useful in public. Instead of telling people your product is good, you show that you understand their problem deeply, and you let that earn the attention. Usefulness is the channel.

This takes a few concrete forms:

- **Teaching content.** Write the post that solves a real problem in your area, the one you wish existed when you hit the issue. Tutorials, teardowns, and honest comparisons.
- **Building in public.** Share what you are building, what broke, and what you learned. Developers trust a visible process more than a finished pitch.
- **Great docs and a real free tier.** For technical products, the documentation and the trial are the marketing. They convert better than any landing page.
- **Direct, specific outreach.** Reaching the right people one at a time, with a message that proves you looked, rather than blasting a list.

The thread connecting these is respect for the reader's intelligence. You are not manufacturing demand with clever copy. You are demonstrating competence and letting technical people draw their own conclusion, which is exactly how they prefer to buy.

## Should You Focus on Content or Outbound?

Both, run together, because they make each other work. Content earns trust at scale and gets you found. Outbound reaches specific people now. A founder who does only one leaves most of the value unclaimed.

Content without outbound is a shop with no one walking past. Outbound without content is a stranger knocking on a door. Together, a cold email to someone who has already read a useful post from you is not really cold, and a post shared with the right person directly travels further. The full mechanics of running both on a small team are in the [go-to-market playbook for solo founders and small technical teams](https://blog.ozigi.app/blog/go-to-market-playbook-solo-founder-technical-team).

The sequencing for a technical founder is usually content as the long game and outbound as the near-term pipeline, both pointed at the same tight audience. Which brings up the audience question, because marketing to "developers" is too broad to act on. Narrowing it is its own skill, covered in [how to define an ICP when your buyers are developers](https://blog.ozigi.app/blog/ideal-customer-profile-for-developer-products).

## How Do You Market Without Sounding Like Marketing?

You market without sounding like marketing by writing the way you would talk to a respected peer: specific, honest about tradeoffs, and free of hype. The moment your content sounds like a brochure, the technical reader stops trusting it.

This is harder than it looks, because most writing tools push you back toward brochure language by default. They reach for words like "robust," "seamless," and "leverage" that signal marketing and trigger the exact distrust you are trying to avoid. The fix is a defined voice and a hard rule against filler. The test is the same one developers apply to your docs: would a knowledgeable person find this useful and honest, or would they smell a pitch? Write for the first answer.

## Where Should Technical Founders Show Up?

Show up where developers already are and already trust the format: GitHub, Dev.to, Hacker News, technical newsletters, and the specific communities around your problem. These are the channels ads cannot reach and where usefulness is rewarded.

The principle is to go to the watering hole rather than buying a billboard near it. A genuinely helpful answer in a community where your buyers hang out does more than a paid campaign aimed at the same people, because it arrives as peer contribution rather than as an interruption they have already chosen to block. The same public footprint that makes these places good to publish in also makes them good to source from, which is why [finding leads on GitHub and Dev.to](https://blog.ozigi.app/blog/how-to-find-b2b-leads-github-devto) and marketing on them are two sides of one motion.

## How Do You Get Found by AI Search?

You get found by AI search by publishing clear, specific, well-structured content that answers real questions, because developers increasingly ask an LLM before they ask Google. Getting cited in those answers is becoming as important as ranking in search results.

This is a real shift in how technical people discover tools. When someone asks an AI assistant "what is the best tool for X," the tools that get named are the ones with clear, citable, genuinely useful content behind them. Optimizing for that is its own discipline, generative engine optimization, covered in our [guide to GEO and AEO](https://blog.ozigi.app/blog/geo-aeo-guide-ozigi). The good news for technical founders is that the content that earns AI citations is the same honest, specific, useful content that earns developer trust. You do not have to choose.

## What Does a Technical Founder's Marketing Week Look Like?

It looks like a small, repeatable loop of a few hours, not a campaign. Publish one useful thing, reach a handful of the right people directly, and engage where your audience already is. Consistency beats intensity.

A workable week: publish one piece of teaching content or a build-in-public update, source and write to 20 to 50 well-matched people with specific messages, spend a little time being genuinely helpful in one community, and review on Friday what earned attention. That is it. The founders who win at marketing are not the ones who ran the biggest launch. They are the ones who showed up usefully, in their own voice, every week, until it compounded.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How is marketing for technical founders different?**
The audience blocks ads at high rates, distrusts polished messaging, and evaluates tools by reading docs and trying them rather than believing claims. So the standard playbook of ads and hype fails. What works is being useful in public, teaching instead of pitching, and reaching people directly in an honest voice.

**Do ads work for developer products?**
Poorly, in most cases. Ad blocker usage among developers and IT professionals runs around 50 to 60%, and developer-community traffic is the most likely to be blocked, so much of an ad budget never reaches the intended audience. Content, community presence, and direct outreach reach technical buyers far more reliably.

**What is the best marketing channel for a technical founder?**
There is no single channel, but the highest-return mix is teaching content plus direct outreach, published and sent where developers already are: GitHub, Dev.to, Hacker News, and your problem's specific communities. Great docs and a real free tier do more converting than any landing page.

**How do I market without sounding salesy to developers?**
Write the way you would talk to a respected peer: specific, honest about tradeoffs, and free of hype words like "robust" and "seamless" that mark a brochure. Show that you understand the problem deeply and let technical readers draw their own conclusion, which is how they prefer to buy.

**Should a technical founder do content or outbound first?**
Both, together. Content is the long game that earns trust and gets you found; outbound is the near-term pipeline. They reinforce each other, since a cold email lands warmer when the person has already read your content. Point both at the same narrow, well-defined audience.
